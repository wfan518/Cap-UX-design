The Big Picture

Heat waves are intensifying. But most climate tools miss the real story: danger isn't evenly distributed.

Your neighbor might be fine while a neighborhood blocks away faces a health crisis. The difference isn't just how hot it gets—it's who lives there and what condition their homes are in.

This tool merges three types of Dutch government data (KlimaatAtlas, CBS, and GGD) to reveal a neighborhood-level portrait of vulnerability across all 11,554 neighborhoods in the Netherlands.

02

The Composite Risk Model

The main "Risk Score" on the dashboard is calculated using a Multiple Linear Regression model. It translates raw statistics—temperature in °C, percentage of elderly residents—into a unified risk estimate trained on historical health outcomes.

The model reveals that temperature is the dominant driver, but it is aggressively amplified by social factors. Once the raw score is computed, the entire dataset is normalized onto a 0–100 scale: 0 is the safest neighborhood; 100 is the most statistically dangerous.

Regression Equation · Linear, trained on CBS/GGD 2024
Risk = −12.4 + +4.32 × Temp (°C) + +4.05 × Elderly (%) + +3.40 × Low-income (%) + +2.18 × Heat island index + +1.74 × Pre-2010 housing (%) − 1.95 × Green space (%)

Understanding the 6 Themes

Because "overall risk" isn't actionable enough for city planners, vulnerability is broken into six distinct themes. These do not use the regression model—instead they use Percentile Ranking across all 11,554 neighborhoods.

What does a score of 82 actually mean?

Theme scores are relative. They tell you exactly how a neighborhood compares to every other neighborhood in the Netherlands.

Safest ← All 11,554 neighborhoods → Most dangerous
0th
This neighborhood scores 82 → higher than 82% of the country
100th
  1. Rank & Percentile: A "Vulnerable Populations" score of 82 means that neighborhood has a higher concentration of at-risk demographic groups than 82% of the country. It sits in the top 18% most dangerous zones for that specific metric.
  2. Inverting Safety Metrics: Some data is recorded as a positive trait (e.g., "percentage of people who can easily find indoor cooling"). The algorithm mathematically inverts these: 100 − Percentile Rank. This ensures that across the entire dashboard, a high number always equals high danger.
  3. Averaging the Variables: If a theme contains multiple variables (like Income, Education, and Rental housing), the algorithm calculates the percentile rank for each individually, sums them, and takes the average. This yields the final 0–100 index shown in the popup tabs.
Theme 01
🌡️
Heat Exposure
What it measures: Physical biometric heat stress. We use Perceived Equivalent Temperature (PET), a biometric standard that factors in solar radiation, wind speed, and humidity alongside the RIVM national risk classification.
Theme 02
👥
Vulnerable Populations
What it measures: People with limited physiological or social resilience. It ranks the concentration of elderly (65+), children (0–14), self-reported physical frailty, and single-person households—a key indicator of social isolation.
Theme 03
💶
Socioeconomic Deprivation
What it measures: Financial barriers. Low-income residents cannot afford AC, high electricity bills, or better insulation. Dependency on rental housing means residents legally cannot modify their structures to protect themselves.
Theme 04
🧠
Mental Health
What it measures: High stress and severe loneliness. Extreme heat disrupts sleep and triggers psychiatric episodes, while isolation impairs cognitive coping—making residents less likely to actively hydrate or seek shade.
Theme 05
🏗️
Built Environment
What it measures: Thermal traps. Buildings constructed prior to 2010 often have poor thermal mass. We use high winter gas usage as a proxy—homes that leak heat in winter will rapidly trap solar gain in summer.
Theme 06
🌳
Access & Green Space
What it measures: Proximity to emergency relief. Distance to GPs dictates the speed of heatstroke treatment, while distance to parks highlights "concrete jungles" that suffer from the Urban Heat Island effect.
04

Using the prototype

The prototype helps municipal users move from neighbourhood-level heat-health data to policy interpretation and possible preventive action.

  1. Locate risk: Identify neighbourhoods with high relative heat-health vulnerability.
  2. Diagnose drivers: See which social, health, environmental, and built-environment factors contribute to vulnerability.
  3. Compare scenarios: Use future scenario views to understand why preventive action may become more urgent over time.
  4. Consider interventions: Connect vulnerability patterns to possible municipal actions, such as cooling spaces, greening, outreach, and housing adaptation.
05

Important Caveats

01
Neighbourhood averages hide internal variation. Within each boundary, pockets of extreme vulnerability exist. Block-by-block risk can be far higher than the neighbourhood score suggests.
02
We assume no behavioural adaptation. In reality, residents will buy air conditioning, relocate, or build mutual support networks. Our long-term projections are deliberately conservative.
03
Health data gaps exist. 11,554 of 14,668 neighbourhoods have complete health data. Any neighborhood lacking sufficient CBS or GGD polling data is excluded from the ranking math to preserve statistical integrity.
