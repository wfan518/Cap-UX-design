Heat does not
discriminate —
but vulnerability does.

As climate change intensifies heat waves across the Netherlands, health risk concentrates in neighborhoods shaped by poverty, aging, social isolation, and housing that traps heat. This tool works as an interactive decision-support prototype for Dutch municipalities and GGD advisors to help identify vulnerable neighbourhoods, explain key risk drivers, and connect heat-health vulnerability to targeted local interventions.

The policy problem

Heat-health risk is uneven, local, and preventable

Rising temperatures do not affect all neighbourhoods equally. Vulnerability is shaped by exposure, age, income, health conditions, housing quality, green space, and access to support.

01

Heat exposure is spatially unequal

Dense urban neighbourhoods with little vegetation and high shares of dark surfaces experience substantially higher heat stress than surrounding areas.

Urban heat is distributed unevenly across neighbourhoods
02

Vulnerability is socially patterned

Elderly residents, children, people with frail health, low-income households, and socially isolated residents face compounding barriers to self-protection.

Heat risk increases where exposure and social vulnerability overlap
03

Housing and infrastructure shape indoor risk

Older housing, limited green space, poor access to cooling locations, and rental constraints can turn heat exposure into a local public-health problem.

Built environments can either reduce or amplify heat-health risk
04

Heat-health risk competes for policy attention

Dutch climate adaptation has long been shaped by collective experience with water and flood protection. Heat-health risks are newer, less visible, and harder to fit into established municipal routines.

The challenge is not only data, but policy interpretation
